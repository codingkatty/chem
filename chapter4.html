Chapter 4: Periodic Table of Elements

Development of Periodic Table of Elements

The Periodic Table of Elements classifies known chemical elements in a table according to certain characteristics. Elements in the Periodic Table of Elements are arranged in ascending order of proton numbers, ranging from 1 to 118, and elements with similar chemical properties are placed in the same vertical columns.

Contributions

Scientist Contribution
Antoine Lavoisier
  • Grouped elements into gas, metal, non-metal, and metal oxide.
  • Included light and heat in classification.
  • Some elements misclassified due to chemical property differences.
Johann W. Dobereiner
  • Grouped elements into triads.
  • Discovered a relationship between relative atomic mass (RAM) within triads.
  • Relationship did not apply to most other elements.
John Newlands
  • Arranged elements by increasing nucleon number.
  • Observed that every 8th element had similar properties (law of octaves).
  • Law failed after the first 16 elements, and no provision for undiscovered elements.
Lothar Meyer
  • Calculated atomic volume by dividing mass by density.
Dmitri Mendeleev
  • Arranged elements by increasing atomic mass.
  • Followed atomic mass, not chemical properties.
Henry G.J. Moseley
  • Bombarded elements with high-energy electrons and measured X-ray frequency.
  • Plotted square root of frequency vs. proton number, obtaining a straight line.

The Arrangement of Elements in the Modern Periodic Table of Elements

How is the Periodic Table of Elements arranged?

  • The elements are arranged in order of increasing proton number from left to right and from top to bottom.
  • The arrangement of elements is discussed from the aspects of groups, periods, proton number, and electron arrangement.
  • The vertical columns in the Periodic Table of Elements are called Groups.
  • There are 18 groups of elements in the Periodic Table of Elements.


You can find the group and period of a element by knowing it's electrons, writing something like 2.8.1, by number of electrons in each shell. The number of shells should be the period, while the valance electron, or the electron number at the last shell shoud be the group. Beware though, if it is 3 or greater than 3, add 10 to it to skip the transition metals.

For example, Na - 2.8.1 - Three shells are there, so period no. is 3 and group 1.

Group 18

Group 18, known as Nobel Gas has 8 valence electrons.

Physical Properties

  • Insoluble in water.
  • Does not conduct electricity.
  • Does not conduct heat.
  • The boiling and melting point is increasing down the group.
  • The van der Waals forces are increasing when the size of the atoms increases.

Chemical Properties

  • Does not react with other elements.
  • Exists as a monatomic (single atom).
  • Inert gases are non-reactive because the valence shell is full.
  • For example, helium has a stable duplet electron arrangement, because the number of valence electrons is two.
  • The other elements in Group 18 have a stable octet electron arrangement because the number of valence electrons is eight.
  • Noble gas did not accept or donate electrons as it is already stable.

  • Used in lighthouse lamps
  • Used for anesthesia

  • Used to treat cancer

Group 1

Group 1, known as akali metals (except hydrogen) have 1 valence electron.

Physical Properties of Group 1 Elements

  • These metals are soft.
  • Good conductors of heat and electricity.

Reactivity

  • Elements are very reactive down the group.
  • They tend to donate electrons and become positive ions.
  • Electron donation helps achieve a stable electron arrangement.
  • The attractive force between the nucleus and valence electron weakens, making electron donation easier.
  • Atomic radius increases down the group, reducing nuclear attraction.

Physical and Chemical Properties of Other Elements in Group 1

  • Soft metals with shiny surfaces and low melting/boiling points.
  • Rubidium and caesium are highly reactive and easily burn.
  • They are often combined with other elements and hard to isolate.
  • Francium is an unstable radioactive isotope with a short half-life.
  • All three elements react vigorously with water and oxygen.

Group 17

Group 17, known as halogens have 7 valence electrons.

Uses of Group 17 Elements

  • Chlorine in bleach.
  • Bromine in fire extinguishers.
  • Iodine as a disinfectant.

Physical Properties of Group 17 Elements

  • Non-metals.
  • Do not conduct heat and electricity.

Period 3 Elements

The elements in Period 3 show a gradual change in physical and chemical properties as you move across the period. The elements in Period 3 are as follows:

Uses of Period 3 Elements

  • Magnesium (Mg) is used as a substance in lighters.
  • Aluminium (Al) is used in cans.
  • Phosphorus (P) is used in fireworks.
  • Sulphur (S) is used as fungicides.

Changes in Physical Properties of Elements Across Period 3

Group 1 2 13 14 15 16 17 18
Element Na Mg Al Si P S Cl Ar

Key Changes Across Period 3

  • Atomic Radius: Decreases across the period due to increased electrostatic force pulling valence electrons closer to the nucleus.
  • Number of Valence Electrons: Increases as you move across the period.
  • Electronegativity: Increases as the atomic radius decreases and the number of protons increases, enhancing the attraction of electrons.
  • Melting and Boiling Points: Generally increase from the left to the middle of the period, then decrease again.
  • Nature of Metals: Changes from metal to metalloid, and then to non-metal as electronegativity increases.
  • Nature of Oxides: Changes from basic to amphoteric and then to acidic across the period.

Uses of Semi-Metallic Elements

  • Metalloids like silicon are used as semiconductors in the manufacture of electronic microchips.
  • These electronic microchips are essential in the production of computers and mobile phones.
